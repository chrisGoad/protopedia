Inheritance

Most programmers will be familiar with the notion of inheritance, but the meaning and utility of the term is general, and not tied to programming specifically. Here is an explanation which does not draw programming into the account.

Of course, in ordinary language, X inherits from Y means that property owned by Y is passed to X when Y dies. In this context, what is meant is different, and no one needs to die. Namely, if X inherits from Y in our sense, it means that whenever Y has a property, such as a color, X will "inherit" that same color, unless explicitly stated otherwise. So in describing X, we don't have to repeat all the information about X that it inherits from Y.

X might inherit from Y and Y in turn from Z. This is what is called a "prototype chain".

Specifically

The structure that gets instantiated is a property/value tree whose nodes are sometimes already connected by prototype chains. Functions may appear in the tree, but only as leaves. Loosely but concisely, I refer to this structure as a "prototype tree".

The instantiation operation takes the tree, and peels off a copy, but only copies interior nodes. Instead of copying atomic data, it anchors the prototype chains of the new nodes back in the counterpart node in the old tree.

Prototype trees have an external representation, and serve as the basis for libraries of infographic components.

Full details on Prototype Trees and the instatiation operator appear in the technical documentation.
