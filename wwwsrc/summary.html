Visibility and Persistence for JavaScript's Prototype Realm

Prototype chains play a central role in JavaScript, but are unseen except by coders1. The chains are transient as well: they live within JavaScript invocations, and disappear when the invocations are complete.

PrototypeJungle, by means of a restriction on the object graph, provides both persistence and UI access to the prototype realm. Application states can be browsed and edited with full access to their prototype structure, and stored with their prototype chains intact. Applications can be assembled from libraries of prototype-enabled components2.

The restriction is to structures that are trees in their object/own-property/value aspect (but which, unlike JSON objects, may contain prototype chains and functions).

The components are objects which obey the same structural rule as the whole application: they must be tree-like in their object/own-property/value aspect. PrototypeJungle provides a serialization method, so the components (and whole application states) can be stored. Components may themselves serve as prototypes, since PrototypeJungle extends instantiation, normally a one-level operation implemented by Object.create or new, to hierarchical structures. The scheme results in highly adjustable applications, as has been seen in the example.

Here is a diagram illustrating the structure of a component or application.

Precisely stated: a red arrow runs from node a to node b if Object.getPrototypeOf(a) === b. A black arrow runs from a to b if a.P === b for some own property P of a. The requirement is that the black and green subgraph (whose edges consist of object/own-property/value relationships), be a tree. Such structures are dubbed, loosely but concisely, "prototype trees".
